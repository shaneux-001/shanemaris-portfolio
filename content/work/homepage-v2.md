The Responsive Homepage was a watershed moment for the Heart Design System. As lead UXD on the project, I guided design work for what became the largest single consumption of HDS at the time of launch — bringing together design tokens, components, and responsive layout patterns into a cohesive, production-ready experience at the front door of southwest.com.

## Context

Prior to this project, southwest.com's homepage had accumulated years of design debt — inconsistent type scales, hand-rolled components that didn't trace back to any shared library, and a layout that predated the company's responsive strategy. The Heart Design System had been established, but it hadn't yet been stress-tested at this scale. The Responsive Homepage was that stress test.

## My role

As lead UXD, I owned the design direction end-to-end: from initial concept through component mapping, stakeholder alignment, and design-to-engineering handoff. I worked closely with the HDS team to identify gaps in the system's coverage — components and patterns that the homepage needed but that hadn't been built yet — and helped prioritize which gaps to close versus work around. This feedback loop between the consuming team and the system team is part of what made HDS stronger after this project.

## The token timing challenge

At the time the project kicked off, Heart DS had components but hadn't fully shipped design tokens — the named, semantic variables that let a change to "brand color" propagate everywhere automatically. We made the call to build against the components as they existed and treat a token follow-up as a fast-follow. That follow-up landed quickly, and the homepage became one of the first experiences to benefit from the full token layer once it shipped.

## Outcome

The Responsive Homepage set the template for how future teams at Southwest would adopt HDS at scale. It demonstrated that the system was production-ready for complex, high-traffic surfaces, and it surfaced a focused list of system improvements that made HDS meaningfully more complete. The project also proved that a single, well-resourced adoption effort could do more to mature a design system than years of incremental contributions.
